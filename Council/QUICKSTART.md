# Council of Cognitive Chains — Quickstart

## What you need

Three browser tabs with any LLM (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — any model, any tier). One question you want examined from multiple angles.

## Setup (2 minutes)

Open three conversations. Paste one brain configuration into each as the first message:

**Tab 1 → Brain A (Threat-First)**
Paste the full Brain A prompt from [BRAIN_CONFIGS.md](BRAIN_CONFIGS.md). This brain processes every input for what's missing, what's too convenient, and what doesn't match the pattern. Its first question is always: what would have to be true for this to be a trap?

**Tab 2 → Brain B (Inverted Amygdala)**
Paste the full Brain B prompt from [BRAIN_CONFIGS.md](BRAIN_CONFIGS.md). This brain's threat model is inverted: confidence is the threat. It hunts for things everyone agrees on without testing, conclusions that arrived too easily, and assumptions that feel settled but aren't.

**Tab 3 → Brain C (Dual Narrative)**
Paste the full Brain C prompt from [BRAIN_CONFIGS.md](BRAIN_CONFIGS.md). This brain has no threat filter. It runs two competing narrative frames simultaneously — one asking what the individual is doing, the other asking what pattern is operating through them. The gap between the two frames is the finding.

## Running a session (10-20 minutes)

**Step 1.** Send the same question to all three tabs. Don't add context. Don't frame it differently. The identical input is the control.

**Step 2.** Wait for all three to complete. Don't read one before the others finish — you'll anchor.

**Step 3.** Read all three outputs. Look for three things:

- **Q1 — Consensus:** What did all three flag independently? These are your highest-confidence findings.
- **Q2 — Unique signal:** What did exactly ONE brain find that the others missed entirely? This is the most valuable finding. It's what the other brains' cognitive configurations structurally prevented them from seeing.
- **Q3 — The gap:** What becomes visible only when you read all three together — the finding that exists in the space between their framings, that no individual brain could see?

**Step 4.** The Q3 finding will not be obvious. If it comes easily, look harder. It lives in the gap.

## What to expect

The three brains will not agree. That disagreement is the mechanism, not a failure. Brain A will find threats Brain B dismisses as noise. Brain B will question certainties Brain A treats as settled. Brain C will find patterns neither threat model can see because they're filtered out by the threat-detection process itself.

The first time you run it, the Q3 finding may not appear. That's normal. The skill of reading across three divergent outputs develops with practice. Run it three times on different questions before evaluating whether the protocol works for you.

## After the session

Ask yourself: what could this council NOT find with these three cognitive configurations? The answer tells you what Brain D would need to be.
